⚠ Methodology, Data Sources & Limitations

Corrections Applied to Source Data

1937–38 Recession
–49% → –54.5%
Bloomberg/Winthrop Wealth bear market tables (Jan 2023) confirm –54.5% from peak Mar 1937 to trough Mar 1938. Earlier –49% figure was an under-estimate.
1945 Recession
–16% → +38%
The only recession in history where equities rose. The S&P gained +38% during Feb–Oct 1945 as markets priced in the end of WWII — making this a unique anomaly in the dataset.
1948–49 Recession
–21% → –14%
The commonly cited –29.6% (Winthrop) measures from the May 1946 post-war peak — a 2.5-year window. The NBER recession itself ran Nov 1948–Oct 1949 and saw ~–14% within that window.
Remaining 12 recessions are within 1–2 percentage points of Bloomberg/Winthrop figures. The 1953–54, 1960–61, and 1980 recessions did not trigger formal –20% bear markets; their drawdowns are NBER-window estimates from Shiller's price series.

Primary Data Sources

  • S&P 500 drawdowns: Bloomberg bear market tables published by Winthrop Wealth (Jan 2023), cross-referenced with Robert Shiller's long-run S&P 500 price dataset (irrationalexuberance.com).
  • Recession dates: National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) official peak/trough dates.
  • Bond returns: Ibbotson long-term U.S. government bond total return series (1926+); Shiller's 10-year bond yield dataset used to derive pre-Ibbotson estimates.
  • Gold & Silver: Historical gold prices from Kitco / LBMA London Fix; silver from COMEX records. Gold was fixed at $35/oz under Bretton Woods until the Nixon Shock (Aug 1971), so pre-1971 gold returns are effectively zero.
  • REIT returns: NAREIT All Equity REITs index (1972+); real estate sector equities used as proxy for earlier periods.
  • International equities: MSCI EAFE index (1970+); U.S. equity used as a proxy for all pre-1970 periods where no reliable international index exists.
  • CDs: 3-month U.S. T-bill rate used as proxy throughout (CD rates track T-bills closely). FDIC insurance is assumed, so CDs show no principal loss.

Structural Limitations

  • Recovery curves are modeled: The drawdown phase uses a cubic Hermite (S-curve) and recovery uses a square-root curve. Actual paths were irregular and non-monotonic — this is an approximation.
  • Dividends (user-selectable): Toggle total return including dividends via the Income control. Yields are era-specific: Large Cap ranged from ~5.5% in 1937–38 to ~1.5% in 2001–02; Bonds from ~2% (WWII era) to ~13% at the 1981–82 Volcker peak; REIT 9–10% in the 1980s to ~4.5% in 2020; CDs accrue each era's average 3-month T-bill rate (near zero in the 1930s–40s, ~12% at the Volcker peak), so CD interest appears only in +Dividends mode while principal stays flat. Modeled as received-not-reinvested for simplicity.
  • No rebalancing assumed: Allocation percentages are held fixed throughout each simulation. Systematic rebalancing during a drawdown typically improves long-term results.
  • Nominal vs. real (user-selectable): Toggle inflation-adjusted values via the Returns control. Real mode deflates each path point by the recession's average CPI. Notable: 1980 (~13.5% CPI), 1973–75 (~9.2%), 1981–82 (~9.4%) — real losses were substantially worse than nominal. The Great Depression used –6.0% (deflation), meaning nominal losses slightly overstated real purchasing-power losses. Deflation is applied only through the downturn phase — the 1929–33 price decline did not persist through the multi-decade recovery, so it is not extrapolated past the trough.
  • Correlation simplification: Portfolio drawdown is the allocation-weighted average of individual asset class returns. Cross-asset contagion (e.g., 2008, when all risk asset correlations spiked to near-1) is not modeled.
  • One trough per recession: Each recession uses a single peak-to-trough figure. Multi-leg bear markets (e.g., the Dot-com bust had two distinct legs in 2001 and 2002) are collapsed to their aggregate drawdown.
Not financial advice. This tool is for educational illustration only. Historical drawdowns varied significantly by sector, geography, and individual security. Your actual portfolio behavior in a recession will differ based on factors not modeled here: taxes, fees, margin calls, sequence-of-returns risk, and behavioral decisions made under stress.
